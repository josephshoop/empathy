The Curriculum

Before building any application, we must interrogate what empathy actually is—deconstructing it as a biological imperative, a phenomenological boundary, and a pre-cognitive physical affect.

1. Game Theory

Understanding systemic incentives and reciprocal altruism via The Evolution of Cooperation (Axelrod) and The Age of Empathy (de Waal).

2. Radical Alterity

Exploring Emmanuel Levinas's "Face of the Other" to critique empathy as ego-driven consumption, focusing instead on infinite responsibility.

3. Affect Theory

Distinguishing precognitive bodily responses from social emotion using Aubrey Anable's Playing with Feelings and Sara Ahmed.

4. Umwelt & Alien Phenomenology

Examining the structural limits of subjective reality and environment through Jakob von Uexküll and Ian Bogost.

5. Literature of Alterity

Testing absolute empathy limits through speculative fiction like Solaris (Lem), Dawn (Butler), and The Left Hand of Darkness (Le Guin).

The Pedagogical Baseline

These traditional texts define standard perspective-taking before students tackle absolute otherness:

  • To Kill a Mockingbird: Establishes the classic "walk in their shoes" cognitive model to be deconstructed.
  • Do Androids Dream of Electric Sheep?: Asks if empathy can be programmed via the Voight-Kampff test.
  • The Empathy Exams: Bridges basic emotional resonance and the labor of Affect Theory.

Interactive Architectures

Synthesizing academic theory by designing "Empathy Modules" that test strict philosophical and empathetic boundaries. These concepts can be deployed as mobile apps, physical boardgames, networked games, or simulated markets.

The Interactive Dilemma

Applying Game Theory: A social computing dilemma demonstrating the mathematics of cooperation and the compounding cost of betrayal.

The Alterity Engine

Applying Levinas & Umwelt: An interactive system resisting player understanding, forcing responses to an Other without comprehending their internal state.

The Affective Dashboard

Applying Affect Theory: A longitudinal feedback loop mapping the circulation of affective responses over time, beyond simple "win/lose" states.
